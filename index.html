H.M. GYANENDRA SHAH

Unity, Sovereignty, and the Future of the Nepalese Throne

2025: The Year of the People's Awakening

Latest: Following the massive pro-monarchy rallies in Kathmandu on March 9, 2025, and the subsequent "Joint People's Movement" launched on March 27, the demand for the restoration of the 1991 Constitutional Monarchy has reached an all-time high. In September 2025, amidst national political unrest, the King called for peace and national healing.

Biography and Legacy

Gyanendra Bir Bikram Shah Dev (born 7 July 1947) served as the King of Nepal from 1950 to 1951 and again from 2001 until 2008. As the second son of Crown Prince Mahendra, he was educated at St. Joseph's in Darjeeling and Tribhuvan University.

Guardian of the Environment: Before his second reign, Gyanendra was a renowned conservationist, serving as chairman of the King Mahendra Trust for Nature Conservation. He remains a symbol of Nepal's natural and cultural preservation.

While his reign faced the challenges of the Nepalese Civil War, supporters today view his 2005 attempt to restore order as a necessary measure for national stability. In 2008, he abdicated with dignity to prevent further bloodshed, handing the sovereign crown back to the people—an agreement that many feel has been broken by modern politicians.

The Restoration Movement

The current federal republic has seen 13 governments in 17 years, leading to economic stagnation and corruption. The people are now looking back to the 240-year rule of the Shah Dynasty for stability.

  • The Democracy Day Address (Feb 19, 2025): The King noted that "the people are tired of endless instability" and urged unity to save the nation.
  • The Grand Welcome (March 9, 2025): Over 10,000 supporters (some estimates say up to 400,000) greeted him at Tribhuvan Airport, chanting "Raja Aau, Desh Bachau!"
  • The 1991 Vision: The movement now demands a return to the 1991 Constitution, featuring a Constitutional Monarchy with a multi-party system and the recognition of Nepal as a Hindu nation.
